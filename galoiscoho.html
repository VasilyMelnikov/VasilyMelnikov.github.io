Galois cohomology in Kummer theory
Let \(G\) be a group. A \(G\)-module is an abelian group \(M\) together with a group action of \(G\) on \(M\) (which I will write multiplicatively, with the group operation of \(M\) written additively). Equivalently, a \(G\)-module is a module over the group ring \(\mathbf{Z}[G]\) of \(G\). This point of view will become useful later since the category of modules over a fixed ring has enough injectives (allowing one to form right derived functors). Consider the functor $$M\longmapsto M^{G},$$ $$M^{G}:=\{x\in M:g\cdot x=x\forall g\in G\}.$$ Elements of \(M^{G}\) are called \(G\)-invariants. Taking \(G\)-invariants is left-exact; it can fail to be right-exact in certain cases (intuitively, the action of \(G\) is less free on quotients). With this in mind, one can define the group cohomology functors \(\{H^{i}(G,\cdot):i\in\mathbb{N}\}\) as the right derived functors of \(M\longmapsto M^{G}\). Although theoretically sufficient, this definition is not easy to compute (being based on injective resolutions). A more accessible definition is obtained by noticing there is an isomorphism $$M^{G}\simeq\mathrm{Hom}(\mathbf{Z},M)$$ where \(\mathbf{Z}\) is given the trivial \(G\)-action, and the Hom is taken in the category of \(\mathbf{Z}[G]\)-modules; this implies $$H^{i}(G,\cdot)\simeq\mathrm{Ext}^{i}_{\mathbf{Z}[G]}(\mathbf{Z},\cdot)$$ for all \(i\).

Lemma (Hilbert's theorem 90). Let \(L\) be a Galois extension of \(K\). Then \(H^{1}\left(\mathrm{Gal}(L/K),L^{\times}\right)=0\).
Proof. Let \(G\) be an arbitrary group. I will first construct the bar resolution of \(G\). Define \(P_{n}\) for integers \(n\geq 0\) inductively: \(P_{0}\) is the free \(\mathbf{Z}\)-module on the set \(G\), with \(P_{n+1}:=P_{n}\otimes_{\mathbf{Z}}P_{0}\); set \(P_{-1}:=\mathbf{Z}\). \(P_{n}\) admits the structure of a \(\mathbf{Z}[G]\)-module, with action $$g\cdot(h_{0}\otimes\dots\otimes h_{n})=gh_{0}\otimes\dots\otimes gh_{n}.$$ The differential \(d_{n}:P_{n}\longrightarrow P_{n-1}\) is the map
$$d_{n}(g_{0}\otimes\dots\otimes g_{n})=\sum_{i=0}^{n}(-1)^{i}g_{0}\otimes\dots\otimes g_{i}g_{i+1}\otimes\dots\otimes g_{n}.$$
One can verify $$\dots\to P_{1}\to P_{0}\to\mathbf{Z}\to 0$$ is long and exact where the connecting maps are the differentials. After applying the Hom functor and removing \(\mathbf{Z}\) from the resulting complex one obtains $$\dots \leftarrow\mathrm{Hom}(P_{1},M)\leftarrow\mathrm{Hom}(P_{0},M)\leftarrow0$$ where \(M\) is a given \(\mathbf{Z}[G]\)-module. Recalling the definition of the Ext functor and noting the original complex was a projective resolution of \(\mathbf{Z}\) yields an isomorphism between the \(i\)-th cohomology of the above complex and \(\mathrm{Ext}^{i}_{\mathbf{Z}[G]}(\mathbf{Z},M)\simeq H^{i}(G,M)\).

Each element of \(\mathrm{Hom}(P_{n},M)\) can be identified in a canonical fashion with a function \(G^{n}\longrightarrow M\) where \(G^{0}\) is a singleton (presumably the identity, although it does not really matter), and vice-versa. Let \(d^{\ast}_{n}\) be the image of \(d_{n}\) under \(\mathrm{Hom}(\cdot,M)\). Call a function \(\varphi:G\longrightarrow M\) a \(1\)-cocycle if the corresponding element \(\xi(\varphi)\) of \(\mathrm{Hom}(P_{1},M)\) satisfies \(d^{\ast}_{1}\circ \xi(\varphi)=0\); denote by \(Z^{1}(G,M)\) the set of \(1\)-cocycles. Then one has

$$H^{1}(G,M)=0\iff \forall\varphi\in Z^{1}(G,M)\exists \psi\in\mathrm{Hom}(P_{0},M):\xi(\varphi)=d^{\ast}_{0}(\psi).$$
Thus it suffices to show the right hand side of the above equivalence for \(G=\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\), \(M=L^{\times}\).

Write \(L\rtimes \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\) for the crossed product algebra, which is the \(L\)-vector space generated by \(G\) endowed with the structure of a \(K\)-algebra with \(xgyh=x(g\cdot y)gh\) for \(x,y\in L\), \(g,h\in \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\). All simple \(L\rtimes \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\)-modules are isomorphic; indeed, \(L\rtimes \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\) is simple as a \(K\)-algebra. Suppose \(\varphi:G\longrightarrow L^{\times}\) is a \(1\)-cocycle. \(L\) (viewed as a vector space over itself) can be endowed with an action of \(L\rtimes \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\) such that \(xg\cdot 1_{L}=x\varphi(g)\) for all \(x\in L\), \(g\in G\) where \(1_{L}\in L\) is the multiplicative identity; write \(N\) for the \(L\rtimes \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\)-module corresponding to this action. Endow \(L\) with the trivial \(L\rtimes \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\)-module structure. By the previous discussion, there is an isomorphism \(u:L\longrightarrow N\) of \(L\rtimes \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\)-modules. Set \(\psi:=u\circ{1_{L}}\); then \(d^{\ast}_{0}\circ \psi=\xi(\varphi)\), as desired.
